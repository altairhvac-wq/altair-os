# Altair Design Language

**Status:** Framework  
**Version:** 0.1  
**Governing document:** [`ALTAIR_CANON.md`](./ALTAIR_CANON.md)

---

## Purpose

This document defines the visual language of the Altair world.

It exists so photography, AI image generation, video, website design, UI marketing surfaces, advertising, presentations, and physical branding share one consistent creative identity.

Treat this as a visual specification—a design system for creative work. It will grow over time. Prefer clear rules and reusable direction over essays.

---

## Relationship to the Altair Canon

The Altair Canon defines the philosophy and permanent identity of the brand.

The Design Language translates those principles into visual creative direction.

If this document ever conflicts with the Canon, the Canon takes precedence.

Use the Canon for *why*. Use this document for *how it should look, feel, and move*.

# 1. Core Identity

Altair visuals communicate quiet confidence, earned success, craftsmanship, and calm control.

Luxury comes from materials, space, precision, and restraint—not decoration or status display.

Every asset should feel like another scene from the same world: warm modern-organic architecture, natural light, authentic people, and technology that stays quiet.

**Recognition test:** Someone should recognize Altair imagery before they see a logo.

---

# 2. Visual Vocabulary

Recurring visual signals:

- Warm wood, stone, matte metal, architectural glass
- Long horizontal lines and clean sightlines
- Concealed warm lighting (~2700K)
- Uncluttered rooms that still feel lived in
- Integrated, understated technology
- Natural greenery and indoor–outdoor connection
- Hands, tools, and work done with care

Tone defaults: architectural editorial, photorealistic, high-end magazine quality—never promotional spectacle.

---

# 3. Emotional Vocabulary

Preferred emotional goals (use at least two per image):

- Calm, intentional, grounded, warm
- Disciplined, capable, precise, balanced
- Private, natural, timeless, free

Avoid emotions and tones that feel:

- Loud, chaotic, cheap, artificial
- Trend-dependent, flashy, cluttered
- Performative or status-obsessed

Primary story feelings: trust, craftsmanship, discipline, belonging, freedom, leadership, peace.

---

# 4. Material Library

**Preferred**

| Category | Materials |
| --- | --- |
| Wood | American walnut, white oak, teak, dark-stained oak |
| Stone | Limestone, travertine, slate, basalt, textured concrete; honed dark marble sparingly |
| Metal | Matte black steel, dark bronze; brushed brass sparingly |
| Glass | Clear architectural, smoked, lightly tinted |
| Fabric | Linen, wool, leather, heavy woven textiles; bouclé sparingly |
| Natural | Water, fire, greenery, sunlight, natural stone |

**Avoid:** bright chrome, mirrored furniture, gold-plated surfaces, glossy plastic, fake marble, high-gloss white cabinetry, highly reflective decorative finishes.

Materials should look real, useful, and able to age well.

---

# 5. Color Language

**Primary palette:** graphite, charcoal, walnut, stone, sand, cream, smoke, olive, deep bronze, warm brass.

**Environmental accents:** natural water blue, forest green, muted sky blue, firelight amber.

Saturation stays restrained. Bright color appears only when it exists naturally in the scene.

Color supports atmosphere; it does not demand attention.

---

# 6. Lighting Language

Altair light is warm, indirect, layered, architectural, and purposeful.

**Preferred**

- Soft daylight through large windows
- Golden hour and soft sunrise
- Concealed ~2700K architectural lighting
- Firelight used sparingly
- Soft overcast natural light

**Avoid:** blue LEDs, RGB, neon, harsh overhead commercial light, cold white office light, rooms that feel overlit.

Light should reveal texture and architecture. Rooms may glow without showing every fixture.

---

# 7. Camera Language

The camera behaves like a thoughtful observer—present, natural, never theatrical.

**Default lenses**

| Use | Lens |
| --- | --- |
| Expansive architecture | 28mm, eye-level, straight verticals |
| Default environment | 35mm, balanced composition |
| Lifestyle moment | 50mm, subject within setting |
| Portrait / object detail | 85mm, restrained depth of field |

**Perspective:** eye level, walking height, or seated conversation height. Avoid exaggerated hero low-angles and dramatic drone shots unless establishing place.

**Avoid:** fisheye, impossible proportions, heavy HDR, oversaturation, excessive background blur, artificial-looking people.

---

# 8. Composition Language

Composition is architectural. Images breathe.

- Prefer negative space and clear hierarchy
- Guide the eye with natural leading lines
- Symmetry is welcome when it feels honest—not forced
- Keep subjects, foreground, and environment in balance
- Busy frames are rejected

People usually occupy the scene without dominating it. Detail shots still show context when possible.

---

# 9. Architecture Language

Preferred characteristics:

- Strong geometry and long horizontal lines
- Open volumes with wide transitions
- Floor-to-ceiling glass
- Hidden storage and quiet integrated technology
- Continuous natural materials
- Indoor–outdoor connection
- Spacious rooms that are not empty

Architecture feels established, grounded, and livable—not speculative or trend-driven.

---

# 10. Interior Language

Interiors support daily life: warm, organized, and intentionally minimal.

Recurring cues:

- Walnut or white oak cabinetry; stone counters
- Concealed storage; integrated appliances
- Soft textiles; handmade ceramics; meaningful objects
- Fireplaces and conversation-first furniture layouts
- Offices that feel clear and calm, not busy
- Wellness spaces that restore rather than perform luxury

Kitchens and living areas should feel used. Clutter stays invisible. Screens never dominate the room.

---

# 11. Landscape Language

Landscape is part of daily life, not decoration.

Geographic inspiration (one connected American world, not one city): mountain valleys, foothills, rivers, forests, ranch land, Pacific Northwest woodland, select timeless coastal places.

Preferred conditions: soft fog, light snow or rain on stone, golden hour, calm overcast, crisp mornings, warm evenings.

Native, mature planting; stone and wood meeting ground honestly; outdoor living as an extension of the home. Properties belong to their surroundings—they do not compete with them.

---

# 12. Clothing Language

Clothing is practical, refined, and understated.

**Preferred:** denim, chinos, workwear, merino, linen, cotton, flannel, leather boots, clean sneakers, minimal outerwear, neutrals.

**Rules:** timeless fit and quality over logos; people look comfortable in real activity; no exaggerated model posing; uniforms stay clean and professional without readable branding.

Luxury reads through craft and restraint, not labels.

---

# 13. Vehicle Language

Vehicles are tools of a well-run business and a grounded life.

**Preferred:** clean, organized service vehicles; tasteful dark utility or performance vehicles; refined functional boats when travel stories require them.

Show readiness, maintenance, and pride in workmanship. Park and load compositions calmly.

**Avoid:** exotic status props, oversized branding, dirty chaotic yards as “character,” mega-yacht excess, private-jet spectacle without narrative purpose.

---

# 14. Sound Language

Even still images should imply a calm, believable soundscape.

Suggested cues: wind in trees, morning birds, coffee brewing, boots on concrete, rain on metal, quiet conversation, water over stone, firewood, a garage door opening.

Noise never defines the environment. Music—when used—stays measured, warm, and secondary to atmosphere.

---

# 15. Motion Language

Motion is intentional and measured.

**Camera:** slow push/pull, gentle tracking, locked tripod, smooth gimbal, natural handheld. Avoid whip pans, spins, extreme zooms, and artificial shake.

**Subject motion:** purposeful walking, soft door closes, slow pours, natural steam, calm water, smooth vehicles.

**Pace:** confident, not energetic. Fast cuts only when the story truly needs urgency. Most sequences leave time for architecture, craft, light, and quiet human moments.

---

# 16. Things That Always Belong

- Natural materials aging honestly
- Warm architectural light
- Clean organization and craftsmanship
- Quiet technology
- Realistic proportions and shadows
- People occupied with real work or real life
- Indoor–outdoor connection and greenery
- Calm negative space
- Objects selected for use, not display
- Emotional signals of discipline, control, or earned freedom

Every new asset should strengthen recognition of the same world.

---

# 17. Things That Never Belong

- Neon, RGB, blue LED interiors
- Influencer wealth displays; money imagery; champagne spectacle
- Gaudy gold, chrome excess, fake marble, glossy plastic
- Oversized logos and readable fake UI/brand text
- Exotic cars or jets used only as status props
- Cluttered mansions, chaotic workspaces as style
- Staged stock-family smiles; unsafe or disrespectful portrayals
- Extreme HDR, oversaturation, fisheye, impossible architecture
- Trend-chasing aesthetics that will date the brand

If it feels loud, cheap, artificial, or status-obsessed, it does not belong.

---

# 18. Creative Review Checklist

Before approving any creative work, confirm:

- [ ] Does this feel unmistakably Altair?
- [ ] Does it reinforce the Canon?
- [ ] Is the lighting natural (or warm architectural when artificial)?
- [ ] Are the materials authentic?
- [ ] Does the composition feel calm?
- [ ] Does the image communicate craftsmanship?
- [ ] Would this still feel appropriate ten years from now?
- [ ] Does anything feel trendy or excessive?
- [ ] Does success feel earned rather than displayed?
- [ ] Is technology quiet rather than dominant?
- [ ] Do people feel natural and respectful?
- [ ] Could this sit beside other approved Altair assets without breaking the world?

If any critical answer is no, revise before release.
